# FUNDING_PLAYBOOK.md

**Status: CONTRACT**

## Purpose

This contract codifies the strategic coaching layer that sits alongside question data collection. The wizard must not just ask *what* — it must coach *how to think about the answer*.

---

## Pillar 1: Storytelling — Show Don't Tell

Every narrative question (`text_long`) that feeds an output slot must encourage the user to share a **specific situation or story**, not list credentials.

### Rules

- **Ban resume mode.** Never prompt "list your experience". Prompt "describe a specific time when…"
- **Coach the switch.** Help text should contrast "telling" vs "showing":
  - Telling: "I have 10 years of experience in community arts."
  - Showing: "In 2022, I ran a three-day printmaking workshop in Hackney with 14 young people who had never held a brayer."
- **Traits over titles.** Encourage users to highlight critical thinking, adaptability, and conflict handling through concrete examples.

---

## Pillar 2: Deep Funder Research

Before writing, users should understand *who* is reading the application.

### Rules

- **Research checklist.** Prompt users to investigate: board members, past winners, their projects, project themes, geographic patterns.
- **Alignment check.** Only apply to grants that align with what you're creating.
- **Anti-Google-Search warning.** Google search surfaces expired grants and winner announcements. Neither helps you.
- **Google Alerts.** Recommend setting up keyword alerts. Better keywords → better leads.

---

## Pillar 3: Eligible ≠ Qualified

Meeting eligibility (age, location, baseline) does not mean you qualify. Qualification is based on internal biases.

### Rules

- **Past-winner analysis.** Before applying, check: geographic patterns, demographic patterns, project-type patterns.
- **Pattern red flags.** If past winners are overwhelmingly from one region, one gender, or one discipline, factor this into your decision.
- **Don't inflate submission counts.** Apply selectively, not broadly.

---

## Pillar 4: Five Facts of Funding

Every funded idea demonstrates these five things. The app must check for all five before the user exports.

1. **Clear outcome** — What specific thing will exist after the money is spent?
2. **Defined audience** — Who exactly will experience or benefit from this?
3. **Realistic scope** — Can you actually do this in the time and budget proposed?
4. **Logical budget** — Do the numbers make sense? Is every line justified?
5. **Proof of capacity** — Can you prove you (or your team) can deliver this?

### Rule

Ideas without architecture get rejected. Funders fund builders, not dreamers.

---

## Pillar 5: Transferable Proof

When the user hasn't done this exact thing before, coach them to demonstrate **transferable traits**.

### Rules

- **Trait mapping.** Identify what characteristics the target project requires (e.g., diverse audiences, high pressure, adaptability) and find past experiences that prove those traits.
- **Adjacent experience.** Managing a school play IS relevant experience for managing an arts festival — if you extract the right traits.
- **Never fabricate.** Only reference real experiences the user provides.

---

## Pillar 6: Keyword Strategy (Grant Discovery)

### Rules

- **Don't use Google Search for grants.** You'll find expired deadlines or winner press releases.
- **Use Google Alerts.** Set up alerts with specific keywords: your discipline + "open call" / "funding" / "grant" / "commission".
- **Refine keywords regularly.** Broader keywords = noise. Specific keywords = leads.
